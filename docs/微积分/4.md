# Infinite Limits and Sequences

## Limit at Infinity

$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to +\infty} f(x) = A
\end{equation*}
$$

means that  

$$
\begin{equation*}
\forall \varepsilon > 0,\; \exists M > 0 \text{ such that } \forall x \in I,\; x > M \Rightarrow |f(x) - A| < \varepsilon.
\end{equation*}
$$


Similarly,  
$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = A
\end{equation*}
$$

means  

$$
\begin{equation*}
\forall \varepsilon > 0,\; \exists M > 0 \text{ such that } \forall x \in I,\; x < -M \Rightarrow |f(x) - A| < \varepsilon.
\end{equation*}
$$

$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = A
\end{equation*}
$$

means  

$$
\begin{equation*}
\forall \varepsilon > 0,\; \exists M > 0 \text{ such that } \forall x \in I,\; |x| > M \Rightarrow |f(x) - A| < \varepsilon.
\end{equation*}
$$


## Deleted Neighborhoods

Let $c$ be one of $x_0,\; x_0^+,\; x_0^-,\; -\infty,\; +\infty,\; \infty$.  The **neighborhood** and **deleted neighborhood** of $c$ are defined as follows:

|    $c$    |          Neighborhood of $c$           |      Deleted neighborhood of $c$       |
| :-------: | :------------------------------------: | :------------------------------------: |
|   $x_0$   | An open interval $J$ containing $x_0$  |         $J \setminus \{x_0\}$          |
|  $x_0^+$  |         $(x_0,\, x_0+\delta)$          |         $(x_0,\, x_0+\delta)$          |
|  $x_0^-$  |         $(x_0-\delta,\, x_0)$          |         $(x_0-\delta,\, x_0)$          |
| $-\infty$ |            $(-\infty,\, b)$            |            $(-\infty,\, b)$            |
| $+\infty$ |            $(a,\, +\infty)$            |            $(a,\, +\infty)$            |
| $\infty$  | $(-\infty,\, b)\,\cup\,(a,\, +\infty)$ | $(-\infty,\, b)\,\cup\,(a,\, +\infty)$ |



## General Definition of Limits

Let $c$ be one of $x_0,\; x_0^+,\; x_0^-,\; -\infty,\; +\infty,\; \infty$,  and let $A$ be a real number or one of $-\infty,\; +\infty,\; \infty$.

We say that  $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = A$  iff: 

For every **neighborhood** $V$ of $A$, there exists a **deleted neighborhood** $W$ of $c$  such that for all $x \in I \cap W$, we have $f(x) \in V$.



## Infinite Quantities

When $x \to c$, if $f(x)$ grows without bound,  we say $f(x)$ is an **infinite quantity** (or **positive/negative infinite quantity**).

If
$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = \infty
\quad \text{(i.e. } \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = +\infty \text{ or } \lim_{x \to c} f(x) = -\infty \text{)},
\end{equation*}
$$
then $f(x)$ diverges to infinity as $x$ approaches $c$.



If
$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = A \in \{-\infty, +\infty, \infty\}, \qquad
\lim_{y \to A} g(y) = B,
\end{equation*}
$$
then
$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to c} g(f(x)) = B.
\end{equation*}
$$

However, if
$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = A, \qquad
\lim_{y \to A} g(y) = B \in \{-\infty, +\infty, \infty\},
\end{equation*}
$$
then 
$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to c} g(f(x)) = B
\end{equation*}
$$
is **not necessarily true**.



## Sequences and Their Limits

A sequence $ \{a_n\}_{n \ge 1} $ can be regarded as a function defined on the set of positive integers $\mathbb{N}^*$.  Hence, its limit can be defined as  
$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{n \to +\infty} a_n.
\end{equation*}
$$


### Theorems

1. Let $\{n_k\}_{k \ge 1}$ be a strictly increasing sequence of positive integers.  Define $b_k = a_{n_k}$.  Then $\{b_k\}_{k \ge 1}$ is called a **subsequence** of $ \{a_n\}_{n \ge 1} $.

  If  
  $$
  \begin{equation*}
  \lim_{n \to +\infty} a_n = A \in \mathbb{R} \cup \{-\infty, +\infty, \infty\},
  \end{equation*}
  $$
  then  
  $$
  \begin{equation*}
  \lim_{k \to +\infty} a_{n_k} = \lim_{k \to + \infty}b_k =  A.
  \end{equation*}
  $$

  

2. Let $f:(a,b) \to \mathbb{R}$ be a **monotone non-decreasing** function.

	- If $f$ is **bounded above**, then  
	  $$
	  \begin{equation*}
	  \lim_{x \to b^-} f(x) = \sup_{x \in (a,b)} f(x).
	  \end{equation*}
	  $$
	  
	- If $f$ is **unbounded above**, then  
	  $$
	  \begin{equation*}
	  \lim_{x \to b^-} f(x) = +\infty.
	  \end{equation*}
	  $$



3. Let $\{a_n\}$ be a **monotone non-decreasing** sequence.

	- If $\{a_n\}$ is **bounded above**, then  
	  $$
	  \begin{equation*}
	  \lim_{n \to +\infty} a_n = \sup_{n \ge 1} a_n.
	  \end{equation*}
	  $$
	  
	- If $\{a_n\}$ is **unbounded above**, then  
	  $$
	  \begin{equation*}
	  \lim_{n \to +\infty} a_n = +\infty.
	  \end{equation*}
	  $$



## Definition of *e*

Let two sequences be defined as  

$$
\begin{equation*}
a_n = \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n, \quad 
b_n = \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^{n+1}.
\end{equation*}
$$

Then:

- $a_n$ is **monotonically increasing**.  
- $b_n$ is **monotonically decreasing**.  
- For all $n$, $a_n < b_n$.  
- Therefore, both sequences converge and have the **same limit**.



We define this common limit as

$$
\begin{equation*}
e = \lim_{n \to +\infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n.
\end{equation*}
$$


### From Discrete to Continuous

$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{x \to \infty} \left( 1 + \frac{1}{x} \right)^x = e.
\end{equation*}
$$



### Theorems

1. $\lim\limits_{x \to 0} \frac{\ln(1+x)}{x} = 1$.
2. $\lim\limits_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$.
3. $\lim\limits_{x \to 0} \frac{a^x - 1}{x} = \ln a$.
4. $\lim\limits_{x \to 1} \frac{x^\mu - 1}{x - 1} = \mu$.
