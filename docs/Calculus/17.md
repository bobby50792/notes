# Improper Integrals

Let $f:[a,+\infty)\to \mathbb{R}$ be Riemann integrable on every bounded closed interval $[a,A]$.

If

$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{A\to+\infty} \int_a^A f(x)\,dx
\end{equation*}
$$

exists, then the **improper integral** $\displaystyle \int_a^{+\infty} f(x)\,dx$ is said to **converge**, and we define

$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_a^{+\infty} f(x)\,dx
=
\lim_{A\to+\infty} \int_a^A f(x)\,dx.
\end{equation*}
$$

If the limit does not exist, the improper integral is said to **diverge**.



Similarly, we define
$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_{-\infty}^a g(x)\,dx
=
\lim_{A\to-\infty} \int_A^a g(x)\,dx.
\end{equation*}
$$


Let $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ be Riemann integrable on every bounded closed interval $[a,b]$.

If both improper integrals

$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_a^{+\infty} f(x)\,dx,
\qquad
\int_{-\infty}^{a} f(x)\,dx
\end{equation*}
$$

converge, then the improper integral

$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f(x)\,dx
\end{equation*}
$$

is said to **converge**, and we define

$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} f(x)\,dx
=
\int_{-\infty}^{a} f(x)\,dx
+
\int_{a}^{+\infty} f(x)\,dx.
\end{equation*}
$$



Let $f:[a,b)\to\mathbb{R}$ be unbounded, but Riemann integrable on every bounded closed interval $[a,b-\delta]$ for $\delta>0$.  In this case, the integral $\displaystyle \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ is called an **improper integral**, and $b$ is called an **improper point**.

If

$$
\begin{equation*}
\lim_{\delta\to 0^+} \int_a^{\,b-\delta} f(x)\,dx
\end{equation*}
$$

exists, then the improper integral $\displaystyle \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ is said to **converge**, and we define

$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx
=
\lim_{\delta\to 0^+} \int_a^{\,b-\delta} f(x)\,dx.
\end{equation*}
$$

Similarly, one may define improper integrals where the left endpoint is an improper point.



**Examples**

1. $$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_{1}^{+\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \mathrm{d}x
	\end{equation*}
	$$

	**Solution:**
	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_{1}^{A} \frac{1}{x^p} \mathrm{d}x =
	\begin{cases}
	\frac{1 - A^{1-p}}{p-1}, & p \neq 1, \\
	\ln A, & p = 1.
	\end{cases}
	\end{equation*}
	$$

	Therefore, the integral converges if and only if $p > 1$. When it converges, the limit is:

	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_{1}^{+\infty} \frac{1}{x^p} \mathrm{d}x = \frac{1}{p-1}
	\end{equation*}
	$$





2. $$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{x^p} \mathrm{d}x
	\end{equation*}
	$$

	**Solution:**
	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_{\delta}^{1} \frac{1}{x^p} \mathrm{d}x =
	\begin{cases}
	\frac{1 - \delta^{1-p}}{1-p}, & p \neq 1, \\
	-\ln \delta, & p = 1.
	\end{cases}
	\end{equation*}
	$$

	Therefore, the integral converges if and only if $p < 1$. When it converges, the limit is:

	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_{0}^{1} \frac{1}{x^p} \mathrm{d}x = \frac{1}{1-p}
	\end{equation*}
	$$



**Unified Definition.** Let $\omega = +\infty$ or let $\omega$ be a improper point of $f$. For any $u \in [a, \omega)$, if $f \in \mathscr{R}[a, u]$, then:
$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_{a}^{\omega} f(x) \mathrm{d}x := \lim_{u \to \omega^-} \int_{a}^{u} f(x) \mathrm{d}x.
\end{equation*}
$$

In this case, $f$ is said to be **improperly integrable** on the interval $[a, \omega)$.



**Theorems**

1. Suppose $\int_{a}^{\omega} f'(x)g(x) \mathrm{d}x$ converges and $\lim_{x \to \omega^-} f(x)g(x)$ converges. Then $\int_{a}^{\omega} f(x)g'(x) \mathrm{d}x$ converges, and:

	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_{a}^{\omega} f(x)g'(x) \mathrm{d}x = f(x)g(x) \bigg|_{a}^{\omega} - \int_{a}^{\omega} f'(x)g(x) \mathrm{d}x.
	\end{equation*}
	$$



## Cauchy Convergence Criterion



**Theorem.** The improper integral $\int_{a}^{+\infty} f(x) \mathrm{d}x$ converges if and only if for any $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N_{\varepsilon} > 0$ such that for any $A_2 > A_1 > N_{\varepsilon}$, the following holds:
$$
\begin{equation*}
\left| \int_{A_1}^{A_2} f(x) \mathrm{d}x \right| < \varepsilon.
\end{equation*}
$$


## Comparison Test for Absolute Convergence and Improper Integral Convergence



**Definition.** An improper integral $\int_{a}^{+\infty} f(x) \mathrm{d}x$ is said to be **absolutely convergent**, or $f$ is absolutely integrable on the interval $[a, +\infty)$, if the improper integral $\int_{a}^{+\infty} |f(x)| \mathrm{d}x$ converges.

From the inequality:

$$
\begin{equation*}
\left| \int_{A_1}^{A_2} f(x) \mathrm{d}x \right| \leq \int_{A_1}^{A_2} |f(x)| \mathrm{d}x
\end{equation*}
$$

It is known that any **absolutely convergent** improper integral is also **convergent**.



### The Gamma Function ($\Gamma$ Function)

For any $\alpha > 0$: 

$$
\begin{equation*}
\Gamma(\alpha) = \int_{0}^{+\infty} x^{\alpha-1}e^{-x} \mathrm{d}x
\end{equation*}
$$

The function satisfies $\Gamma(\alpha+1) = \alpha\Gamma(\alpha)$ and $\Gamma(1) = 1$. Therefore, for any positive integer $n$:

$$
\begin{equation*}
\Gamma(n+1) = n!
\end{equation*}
$$


### The Beta Function ($B$ Function)

For any $\alpha > 0, \beta > 0$:

$$
\begin{equation*}
B(\alpha, \beta) = \int_{0}^{1} x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1} \mathrm{d}x
\end{equation*}
$$

It satisfies $B(\alpha+1, \beta) = \frac{\alpha}{\beta} B(\alpha, \beta+1)$.



The relationship between the **Beta function** and the **Gamma function** is given by the following identity:
$$
\begin{equation*}
B(\alpha, \beta) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}
\end{equation*}
$$


**Definition.** An improper integral is said to be **conditionally convergent** if it converges but is not absolutely convergent.



### Theorems

1. Let $f$ be Riemann integrable on any bounded closed interval $[a, A]$, and let $g$ be monotonic. If:

	* $\int_{a}^{A} f(x) \mathrm{d}x$ is bounded for all $A > a$,
	* $\lim_{x \to +\infty} g(x) = 0$,

	then the improper integral $\int_{a}^{+\infty} f(x)g(x) \mathrm{d}x$ converges.



2. Let $f$ be Riemann integrable on any bounded closed interval $[a, A]$, and let $g$ be monotonic. If:

	* The improper integral $\int_{a}^{+\infty} f(x) \mathrm{d}x$ converges,
	* $g(x)$ is bounded on the interval $[a, +\infty)$,

	then the improper integral $\int_{a}^{+\infty} f(x)g(x) \mathrm{d}x$ converges.
