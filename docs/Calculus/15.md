# Properties and Computation of Definite Integrals

## Basic properties of the definite integral

1. Assume that $f, g \in \mathcal{R}[a,b]$. Then for any real numbers $\lambda, \mu$,  we have $\lambda f + \mu g \in \mathcal{R}[a,b]$, and moreover,
	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_a^b [\,\lambda f(x) + \mu g(x)\,]\, dx
	= \lambda \int_a^b f(x)\, dx
	+ \mu \int_a^b g(x)\, dx.
	\end{equation*}
	$$



2. If $f$ is Riemann integrable on $[a,b]$ and on $[b,c]$,  then $f$ is integrable on $[a,c]$, and
	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_a^b f(x)\,dx + \int_b^c f(x)\,dx
	= \int_a^c f(x)\,dx.
	\end{equation*}
	$$



3. $$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_b^a f(x)\,dx = - \int_a^b f(x)\,dx.
	\end{equation*}
	$$



4. Assume $f, g \in \mathcal{R}[a,b]$, and $f(x) \le g(x)$ for all $x \in [a,b]$,  and the set $\{x \in [a,b]\mid f(x) < g(x)\}$ has positive length.  Then
	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_a^b f(x)\,dx < \int_a^b g(x)\,dx.
	\end{equation*}
	$$



5. Assume $f \in \mathcal{C}[a,b]$, $g \in \mathcal{R}[a,b]$, and $g(x) \ge 0$ for all $x \in [a,b]$.  Then there exists $\xi \in [a,b]$ such that
	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_a^b f(x)g(x)\,dx
	= f(\xi)\int_a^b g(x)\,dx.
	\end{equation*}
	$$



6. Let $f, g \in \mathcal{R}[a,b]$. Then

	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\left|
	\int_a^b f(x)g(x)\,dx
	\right|
	\,\le\,
	\sqrt{\int_a^b |f(x)|^2\,dx}\;
	\sqrt{\int_a^b |g(x)|^2\,dx}.
	\end{equation*}
	$$



## Fundamental theorem of calculus

1. Assume that $f \in \mathcal{R}[a,b]$. Define

$$
\begin{equation*}
F(x) = \int_a^x f(u)\,du.
\end{equation*}
$$
Then $F \in \mathcal{C}[a,b]$.



2. If $f \in \mathcal{R}[a,b]$ and $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$, then

	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_a^b f(u)\,du = F(b) - F(a).
	\end{equation*}
	$$



## Taylor's formula with integral remainder

If $f^{(n+1)} \in \mathcal{R}[a,b]$, then for every $x \in [a,b]$,

$$
\begin{equation*}
f(x)
=
f(a)
+
f'(a)(x-a)
+
\cdots
+
\frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!} (x-a)^n
+
\int_{a}^{x}
\frac{f^{(n+1)}(t)}{n!} (x - t)^n \, dt.
\end{equation*}
$$




## Simpson's rule

The integral
$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\, dx
\end{equation*}
$$

is approximated by Simpson's formula:

$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_{a}^{b} f(x)\, dx
\;\approx\;
\left[
f(a)
+ 4 f\!\left( \frac{a+b}{2} \right)
+ f(b)
\right]
\frac{b-a}{6}.
\end{equation*}
$$


## Theorems

1. Suppose that $f$ is continuous on an interval $I$,  $a, b \in I$, and $\varphi : [\alpha, \beta] \to I$ satisfies  $\varphi(\alpha) = a$, $\varphi(\beta) = b$, and $\varphi' \in \mathcal{R}[\alpha, \beta]$.  Then

$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx
= \int_\alpha^\beta f(\varphi(t))\, \varphi'(t)\, dt.
\end{equation*}
$$



2. If $f', g' \in \mathcal{R}[a,b]$, then

	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_a^b f(x) g'(x)\,dx
	= f(b)g(b) - f(a)g(a)
	- \int_a^b g(x) f'(x)\,dx.
	\end{equation*}
	$$





## Examples

1. Compute
	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \frac{x}{\sin x + \cos x}\, dx.
	\end{equation*}
	$$

​	


$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \frac{x}{\sin x + \cos x}\, dx
= \int_{\frac{\pi}{2}}^{0}
\frac{\frac{\pi}{2} - x}{\cos x + \sin x}\, d\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2} - x\right)
= \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} 
\frac{\frac{\pi}{2} - x}{\sin x + \cos x}\, dx.
\end{equation*}
$$

Define
$$
\begin{equation*}
I = \frac{1}{2}\left(
\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \frac{x}{\sin x + \cos x}\, dx
+
\int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}} \frac{\frac{\pi}{2} - x}{\sin x + \cos x}\, dx
\right).
\end{equation*}
$$

Then
$$
\begin{equation*}
I = \frac{\pi}{4} \int_0^{\frac{\pi}{2}}
\frac{1}{\sin x + \cos x}\, dx.
\end{equation*}
$$

Since
$$
\begin{equation*}
\sin x + \cos x = \sqrt{2}\,\cos\!\left(x - \frac{\pi}{4}\right),
\end{equation*}
$$
we get
$$
\begin{equation*}
I = \frac{\pi}{4\sqrt{2}}
\int_{-\frac{\pi}{4}}^{\frac{\pi}{4}}
\frac{d(\sin t)}{1 - \sin^2 t}.
\end{equation*}
$$

Therefore
$$
\begin{equation*}
I = \frac{\pi}{4\sqrt{2}}
\ln\left(\frac{2+\sqrt{2}}{2-\sqrt{2}}\right).
\end{equation*}
$$




2. Compute the limit
	$$
	\begin{equation*}
	\lim_{n \to +\infty} \left( \frac{1}{n+1} + \frac{1}{n+2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{4n} \right).
	\end{equation*}
	$$


$$
\begin{equation*}
\frac{1}{n+1} + \frac{1}{n+2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{4n}
= 
\frac{1}{n} \left[
\frac{1}{1 + \frac{1}{n}}
+
\frac{1}{1 + \frac{2}{n}}
+
\cdots
+
\frac{1}{4}
\right].
\end{equation*}
$$

​	As $n \to \infty$, this Riemann sum converges to the integral

$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_{1}^{4} \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \ln 4.
\end{equation*}
$$


3. Find the asymptotic expression of  $\ln(N+1) + \ln(N+2) + \cdots + \ln(2N)$
	as $N \to +\infty$.



​	Consider the Riemann sum
$$
\begin{equation*}
\frac{1}{N}
\left(
\ln\frac{N+1}{N}
+
\ln\frac{N+2}{N}
+
\cdots
+
\ln\frac{2N}{N}
\right)
\;\longrightarrow\;
\int_{1}^{2} \ln x \, dx.
\end{equation*}
$$

​	Compute the integral:

$$
\begin{equation*}
\int_{1}^{2} \ln x \, dx
=
x \ln x \Big|_{1}^{2}
-
\int_{1}^{2} x \, d(\ln x)
=
2 \ln 2 - 1.
\end{equation*}
$$

​	Therefore,

$$
\begin{equation*}
\ln(N+1) + \ln(N+2) + \cdots + \ln(2N)
=
N \ln N + (2\ln 2 - 1) N + o(N).
\end{equation*}
$$
